Generalizable Dynamics Models for Deformable Objects

Tool-Agnostic Model for Tool Geometry Design

Nielsen Cugito1,2, Kelsey R. Allen1,2
1University of British Columbia, Department of Computer Science, 2Vector Institute
Eurographics Symposium on Geometry Processing (SGP) 2026 · Computer Graphics Forum, Vol. 45 (2026), No. 5
2-Page Abstract Poster BibTeX

A single dynamics model is trained on a variety of tool–dough interactions and generalizes to tools whose shapes are unseen during training (top). Its differentiability lets us optimize a tool's geometry so that rolling out with the optimized tool matches a target dough shape (bottom).

Abstract

Physics-based simulation of deformable objects is often too inaccurate for complex, contact-rich interactions in real-world settings, while learned simulators often suffer from hallucinations when making predictions outside their training distribution.

Here we present an approach for learning deformable object dynamics directly from real-world RGB-D observations, rather than synthetic simulation data, accurate enough to drive the computational design of geometry. Our method uses graph neural networks to learn a single dynamics model spanning a variety of tool–object interactions from RGB-D data alone. Surprisingly, we find that training on a small set of tool geometries yields better in- and out-of-distribution generalization than training on a single tool, and can plausibly predict interactions with tools entirely unseen during training. We further exploit the differentiability of the learned model to optimize tool geometry, creating novel tools to solve a variety of downstream tasks.

Real-World Data Collection

Dough and tool point clouds are fused from a multi-view depth camera setup as a robot arm manipulates dough with different 3D-printed tools — no simulator and no point-to-point correspondences over time.

GNN Dynamics Model

A graph neural network predicts per-particle motion of the dough from the current dough state and tool particles (with history). Graph connectivity is recomputed at every timestep via fixed-radius search, and the representation is translation-invariant — it contains no absolute positions — which improves generalization. The model is trained with a Chamfer Distance + Earth Mover's Distance loss, rolled out over multiple future timesteps to stabilize long-horizon predictions.

Tool Shape Optimization

Because the dynamics model is differentiable, we can optimize a tool's geometry directly. Tool shape is represented as oriented points via the Shape-as-Points representation, converted to a mesh with a differentiable Poisson solver (DPSR).

Given an initial tool shape, a dough state, and an action sequence, we roll out the GNN and compare the resulting dough shape to a target with a 3D loss (Chamfer + Earth Mover's Distance). We backpropagate this loss through the GNN rollout and through DPSR with Adam, updating the tool's surface points and normals — a gradient-based approach that scales far better to high-dimensional design spaces than sampling-based tool design.

Generalization to Unseen Tools

Trained only on push and pinch tools, the multi-tool model is evaluated on two tool geometries it never saw during training.

Conclusion

  • Multi-tool training, with noise injection into tool particle histories, generalizes to unseen tool geometries.
  • Improved shape reconstruction and fewer tool–dough interpenetrations on real data.
  • A differentiable dynamics model enables high-dimensional tool-shape design, without hallucinating shapes that outperform what the model was trained on.
